Few financial planning topics generate as much enthusiasm — and as much confusion — as the Roth conversion. The idea is appealing: pay tax now on Traditional IRA money at today's rates, then watch that money grow tax-free forever. In practice, whether it's a good idea depends on a half-dozen variables most articles ignore. Here's the framework we use.

## The core question

A Roth conversion is essentially a bet on your future tax rate. If your marginal rate in retirement will be *higher* than your rate today, conversion wins. If it will be *lower*, conversion loses. If it's the same, you're roughly neutral — and the friction of paying taxes today usually makes "neutral" a slight loss.

The complication: nobody knows their future tax rate. We work the problem from multiple angles.

## When conversions usually make sense

**1. The gap year.** A retiree who has stopped working but hasn't started Social Security or RMDs (Required Minimum Distributions) often spends two to five years in a remarkably low tax bracket. This is the single best conversion window in most people's lives. Filling up the 12% and 22% brackets at $0 of earned income is a layup.

**2. Pre-RMD planning for large IRAs.** A 65-year-old with a $3M Traditional IRA will face significant RMDs at age 73 — and those distributions can push the retiree into higher Medicare premium tiers (IRMAA) for decades. Deliberate, multi-year conversion in the 60s can flatten the lifetime tax curve dramatically.

**3. Anticipated higher future rates.** If you genuinely believe federal rates are heading up — perhaps due to the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act sunset — converting at today's known rates is a hedge against unknown future rates.

**4. Estate planning for heirs in high brackets.** Inherited Traditional IRAs must now be drained within 10 years under the SECURE Act. If your beneficiaries are in their peak earning years, they may pay 32–37% on every distribution. Converting at your 24% rate is, in effect, paying their tax bill at a discount.

## When conversions usually don't make sense

**1. You'll pay the tax from the IRA itself.** A conversion paid for from the converted dollars is dramatically less efficient. The conversion only really wins when the tax can be paid from outside funds.

**2. Your retirement income will be lower than your working income.** This is the default case for most professionals. If you expect to be in the 22% bracket in retirement, converting at 32% today is destroying value.

**3. You're close to a means-tested cliff.** Conversions raise AGI, which can affect ACA subsidies, financial aid, and IRMAA brackets. The "tax cost" of a conversion is sometimes higher than the marginal rate suggests.

**4. You may need the money soon.** Roth conversions have a 5-year clock for the converted principal. If you'll need the dollars within five years, the conversion creates more problems than it solves.

## How we actually evaluate

For each client we model the next 20–30 years of projected income, taxes, RMDs, and Medicare premiums under two scenarios: convert and don't convert. We look at lifetime taxes paid, not just this year's bill. Sometimes the right answer is "convert $80,000 per year for the next four years." Sometimes it's "convert nothing." Sometimes it's "convert aggressively in 2025, then stop." The math doesn't care about the elegance of the strategy. It cares about your specific numbers.
