By the time most professionals reach their 40s, income has accelerated, responsibilities have multiplied, and a comfortable lifestyle feels permanent. It often isn't. After two decades of working with high earners, we see the same five mistakes repeatedly. They rarely look like mistakes in the moment — they feel like success.

## 1\. Confusing income with wealth

High earners often equate their salary with financial security. But wealth is what's left after taxes, lifestyle creep, and underperforming investments. A $750,000 income paired with $740,000 in annual spending creates almost no wealth — it just produces a beautiful lifestyle that requires the income to continue indefinitely. We've seen executives in their late 50s realize, with shock, that the lifestyle they built can't survive a retirement transition.

## 2\. Under-funding their 401(k) for "liquidity reasons"

Many high earners contribute the minimum to capture the company match and hold the rest in taxable brokerage accounts because "you never know when you'll need it." Over 25 years, the cost of foregone tax-deferred compounding can easily reach seven figures. The "liquidity premium" rarely justifies that.

## 3\. Carrying too much in employer stock

When a meaningful share of net worth is tied to the same company that pays your salary, you have a concentration risk problem on top of a job risk problem. RSUs and ISOs accumulate quietly. We routinely meet clients whose net worth is 40–60% in a single stock — often without realizing it.

## 4\. Skipping estate planning because "we're not old enough"

A 45-year-old couple with two kids, a house, retirement accounts, and life insurance is exactly the household estate planning was designed for. The reason isn't fear of death — it's clarity about guardians, trustees, beneficiaries, and incapacity. Without documents, the state decides. With them, you do.

## 5\. Hiring the wrong kind of advisor

The financial services industry is bifurcated. Some "advisors" earn commissions on products they sell to you. Others, like fiduciary fee-only firms, are paid only by the client and are legally required to act in your interest. The difference compounds quietly over decades. Ask any advisor a single question: "How are you paid?" If the answer requires more than two sentences, look elsewhere.

## The course-correction

None of these mistakes is fatal in your 40s. All of them get harder to fix in your 50s, and harder still in your 60s. The decade between 40 and 50 is the most leveraged period in most people's financial lives — the place where small, deliberate decisions about saving rates, tax strategy, and asset location quietly compound into the difference between optional retirement and required retirement. The work isn't dramatic. It's just systematic.
