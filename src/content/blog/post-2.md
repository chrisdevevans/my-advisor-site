Retirement is the only project most people will undertake where they spend down a portfolio they spent forty years accumulating. The psychological shift is harder than the math. Watching a balance go down — even when it's supposed to — creates a kind of low-grade panic that good planning should prevent. The bucket strategy is one of the simplest, most durable frameworks we use to make that easier.

## What a "bucket" actually is

A bucket is just an account or sub-portfolio with a defined time horizon and investment posture. Most plans we build use three:

**Bucket 1 — The Now Bucket (years 1–2 of spending)**
Cash, money market funds, very short-term Treasuries. The job here isn't growth; it's certainty. Whatever happens in markets next year, this money is here.

**Bucket 2 — The Soon Bucket (years 3–7)**
High-quality short-to-intermediate bonds, possibly a small slice of dividend equities. Some growth, but volatility is muted. This bucket exists to refill Bucket 1.

**Bucket 3 — The Later Bucket (years 8+)**
The traditional growth portfolio: globally diversified equities, perhaps some real estate or alternative assets. This is where long-term wealth lives. It can be volatile because you won't touch it for nearly a decade.

## Why it works

The strategy works for two reasons — one mathematical, one psychological.

Mathematically, it solves sequence-of-returns risk. A retiree who is forced to sell stocks during a 30% bear market in their first retirement year may never recover. With buckets, you don't sell stocks when stocks are down — you spend from cash, then refill from bonds when conditions allow. The portfolio gets time to recover.

Psychologically, it gives the retiree a clear answer to the most anxious question in retirement: *"Am I going to be okay?"* The answer is, "Yes, you have two years of spending in cash regardless of what markets do." That single sentence resolves more late-night worry than any spreadsheet.

## What it isn't

The bucket strategy is a *framework*, not a magic formula. Returns still matter. Withdrawal rates still matter. Tax location still matters. And buckets need to be refilled deliberately — typically once a year — not reactively.

## When we refill

Our default rule: at the end of each year, if equities have had a good year, we trim from Bucket 3 and refill Bucket 2 (and from there, Bucket 1). If equities have had a bad year, we leave Bucket 3 alone and refill Bucket 1 from Bucket 2. The bucket framework gives you permission to wait. Permission to wait is, in retirement, one of the most valuable things a portfolio can provide.

Retirement isn't a single decision. It's thirty years of small decisions. A framework that simplifies those decisions — and reduces the emotional pressure on each one — is the quiet difference between a retirement that works and one that doesn't.
